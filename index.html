Why this matters

A crash is never just a dot on a map. It is a person crossing after work, a cyclist squeezed beside traffic, a driver navigating rain and fading light, or a neighborhood shaped by infrastructure decisions made years earlier. When collisions repeat in the same places, they suggest something larger than chance.

This project looks across Seattle collision records from 2022 to 2026 to understand where risk concentrates, what conditions are associated with it, who bears the impact, and when danger peaks over the course of the day. Rather than treating crashes as isolated incidents, we approach them as a citywide pattern that can be examined, compared, and acted on.

Stop 1 · Spatial concentration
Where do crashes cluster citywide?

The first question is geographic. Some crashes are spread out, but others repeatedly cluster in the same corridors, intersections, and high-volume areas.

Looking at the city as a whole helps separate isolated incidents from recurring hotspots. A dense cluster can point to heavy traffic, complex road geometry, frequent turning conflict, or street conditions that make mistakes more likely and more costly.

This stop asks you to read Seattle not as a uniform grid, but as a landscape of uneven risk. Where collisions pile up, safety interventions may matter most.

Stop 2 · Contributing conditions
What conditions tend to surround crash risk?

A hotspot alone does not explain why collisions happen. To understand risk, we also need to look at the conditions recorded around them.

Weather, lighting, and road surface conditions do not all matter in the same way. Rain may be common simply because Seattle is rainy. Darkness may affect visibility differently across travel modes. Road conditions may become more revealing when we isolate severe crashes.

This stop compares which contextual factors appear most often and how those patterns shift when you narrow the view to different years, times of day, and crash types.

Move from where risk appears to what may be shaping it.
Stop 3 · Human impact
Who is affected when streets become unsafe?

Crash counts can make safety feel abstract. This stop shifts attention back toward the people who experience risk differently.

Not every road user faces the same level of exposure or vulnerability. A person inside a car, a cyclist in a painted lane, and a pedestrian crossing a busy street each encounter different physical consequences even within the same environment.

This section highlights how impact is distributed across travel modes and asks whether some users carry a disproportionate share of the burden.

Stop 4 · Time patterns
When does crash risk intensify across the day and week?

Streets do not behave the same way at all hours. Commuting waves, nightlife, congestion, and visibility all change the rhythm of risk.

Looking at timing helps us move beyond static maps. A corridor that seems average across a full year may still become dangerous during a narrow evening window or during recurring rush periods.

This stop is about rhythm: not just where danger exists, but when it peaks.

Stop 5 · Road context
What does road context suggest about safer street design?

Once patterns are visible, the next question becomes practical. What features of the street environment are associated with safer or more dangerous outcomes?

Speed, street type, and surrounding infrastructure all shape what happens when conflict occurs. A road built for higher speeds leaves less room for error. Safety tools such as traffic calming, clearer crossings, and more protective design can change what would otherwise become a predictable crash site.

This stop connects the data back to road design choices and the possibility of prevention.

Stop 6 · Conclusion
What should readers take away from the route?

By the end of the route, the goal is not just to know more facts about Seattle crashes. It is to see how those facts connect.

Hotspots show where harm repeats. Contributing conditions suggest when risk intensifies. Mode breakdowns reveal who is most vulnerable. Road context points toward the role of design. Together, these patterns make crashes feel less random and more explainable.

A safer city is not built by reacting to every collision one at a time. It is built by recognizing repeated patterns and acting before the next one happens.
